1 · Reflection — The Law of Reflection
Core definitions
Key terms — reflection
NormalA line drawn perpendicular (90°) to the surface at the exact point where the light ray meets it. All angles in optics are measured from the normal — never from the surface itself.
Incident rayThe ray of light travelling towards the reflecting surface, before it bounces off.
Reflected rayThe ray of light bouncing away from the reflecting surface after reflection.
Angle of incidence (i)The angle between the incident ray and the normal. Measured from the normal.
Angle of reflection (r)The angle between the reflected ray and the normal. Always equals the angle of incidence.
i = r

The Law of Reflection:
"The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. Both angles are measured from the normal at the point of incidence. The incident ray, normal and reflected ray all lie in the same plane."

⚠ Angles are ALWAYS measured from the NORMAL — not from the mirror surface. Measuring from the surface gives the complementary angle (90° − i), which is wrong.

Regular vs Diffuse Reflection

Law of reflection still holds at every point on a rough surface — but the surface is uneven so rays scatter in all directions.

Images in plane mirrors
Properties of images in a plane (flat) mirror
  • Virtual — light only appears to come from the image position; no light rays actually pass through it
  • Same size as the object (magnification = 1)
  • Same distance behind the mirror as the object is in front
  • Upright — not inverted vertically
  • Laterally inverted — left and right are swapped

Object distance = Image distance from mirror

To locate an image using ray diagrams: draw two rays from the object to the mirror (applying law of reflection for each). Extend the reflected rays behind the mirror with dashed lines. Where they meet = image position.
2 · Refraction — Bending of Light
What is refraction and why does it happen?
Refraction — definition and cause
RefractionThe change in direction (bending) of a wave as it crosses the boundary between two different media, caused by a change in the wave's speed.
Why it happensLight travels at different speeds in different materials. In a vacuum/air: 3 × 10⁸ m/s. In glass: ~2 × 10⁸ m/s. In water: ~2.25 × 10⁸ m/s. Slowing down at an angle causes bending.
Angle of refractionThe angle between the refracted ray and the normal at the boundary, measured inside the new medium.
Optical densityA measure of how much a medium slows light down. Glass is optically denser than air; air is optically denser than vacuum (barely).
Air → Glass (denser)Light slows down → bends TOWARD the normal. Angle of refraction < angle of incidence.
Glass → Air (less dense)Light speeds up → bends AWAY from the normal. Angle of refraction > angle of incidence.
Along the normal (i = 0°)Light hits the boundary at exactly 90° — it passes straight through with no bending at all.
Lateral displacementAfter passing through a parallel-sided block, the emergent ray is parallel to but displaced sideways from the incident ray.
⚠ Refraction is caused by a change in SPEED, not just a change in medium. If a wave crosses a boundary at 90° (along the normal), it changes speed but does NOT change direction.

3 · Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
Total Internal Reflection
Critical angle (C)The angle of incidence (in the denser medium) at which the angle of refraction = 90°. The refracted ray travels along the boundary. For glass: C ≈ 42°. For water: C ≈ 49°.
TIR conditions1) Light must be travelling from a denser to a less dense medium (e.g. glass to air). 2) The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle (i > C).
What happensALL the light is reflected back into the denser medium — no refracted ray exits. The law of reflection applies to this reflected ray.
Optical fibresThin glass fibres use TIR to carry infrared/light pulses. The ray hits the glass-cladding boundary at greater than the critical angle → TIR keeps the light inside the fibre. Used for internet, phone calls, endoscopes.
DiamondsVery small critical angle (~24°) → most rays undergo TIR many times → lots of internal reflections → sparkle.
Periscopes / binocularsUse 45° glass prisms instead of mirrors. TIR gives almost 100% reflection (better than mirrors which absorb some light).

4 · Real-Life Applications & Examples

Refraction in everyday life

Straw in waterStraw appears bent at the water surface. Light from the straw bends away from normal as it exits the water — your brain traces it back in a straight line to the wrong position.
Apparent depthA pool looks shallower than it is. Light bends away from normal leaving water → our brain places the object higher than it really is.
RainbowWhite light dispersed (split into colours) by refraction and TIR inside raindrops. Red refracts least; violet refracts most.
LensesConvex lens: converges parallel rays to a focal point (used in cameras, eyes, magnifying glasses). Concave lens: diverges rays.

Reflection in everyday life

Plane mirrorsRegular reflection from a smooth flat surface. Virtual, upright image same size and distance behind mirror.
PeriscopesUse two mirrors (or prisms using TIR) to allow viewing around corners. Each mirror deflects light by 90°.
Cat's eyes (road)Use retro-reflectors — light returns to its source regardless of angle. Two small prisms reflect light back to car headlights.
TelescopesReflecting telescopes use concave mirrors to collect and focus light from distant objects.
5 · Snell's Law & Refractive Index
Snell's Law — quantifying refraction
n = sin i ÷ sin r
n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
nRefractive index (no units). Ratio of sin(angle of incidence) to sin(angle of refraction).
sin iSine of the angle of incidence (in air/vacuum)
sin rSine of the angle of refraction (in the medium)
Refractive indexA measure of how much a medium slows light. n = speed in vacuum ÷ speed in medium = c ÷ v.
Glassn ≈ 1.5. Light slows to 2 × 10⁸ m/s.
Watern ≈ 1.33. Light slows to 2.25 × 10⁸ m/s.
Air / vacuumn = 1.0. Light travels at 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
Critical anglesin C = 1/n. For glass: sin C = 1/1.5 = 0.667, so C ≈ 42°.

Worked example: A ray enters glass at an angle of incidence of 40°. The angle of refraction is 25°. Calculate the refractive index.
n = sin i ÷ sin r = sin 40° ÷ sin 25° = 0.643 ÷ 0.423 = 1.52 (glass ✓)
